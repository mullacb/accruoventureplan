2.6 · The competitive picture

The competitive picture. In Ireland, and across the EU.

Three groups compete in Irish workplace pensions today: digital disruptors, broker networks, and legacy providers. Each is held back by a different constraint. Across the EU, the pattern is similar: digital pension platforms exist, but none combine workplace distribution, EuroPension portability, and the broader benefits package.

Ireland
01 · Digital disruptors

Insurance brokers in digital wrappers.

Kota (Series A 2025) and Zen Pensions (launched Jan 2026) resell PRSAs from manufacturers like Acorn or Aviva. They take a broker commission plus an AUM charge and don't manufacture the product, so the fee structure is capped by the model.

02 · Broker networks

Fragmented, consolidating revenue, not digital.

The Irish broker market remains very fragmented (around [STAT NEEDED] pension intermediaries on the Pensions Authority register). Where consolidation is happening, it is rolling up books of revenue, not investing in digital.

03 · Legacy providers

Caught between technology and channel.

Irish Life, Zurich, New Ireland manufacture pensions but distribute through brokers (~80% of revenue). They rarely go direct-to-consumer; when they do, the digital experience is poor and reserved for the largest clients.

Heard from the Head of Investments at Bank of Ireland
“We've had multiple conversations with Kota about distributing New Ireland products on their platform. We don't view them as a competitor, they're another broker of our products.”
Head of Investments, Bank of Ireland (paraphrasing the New Ireland CFO)
EU competition

Single-benefit digital providers exist across Europe but none combine workplace distribution, EuroPension portability, and the broader benefits package. Epsor in France runs a B2B pension platform but doesn't offer EuroPension and doesn't extend into health and life cover. Finax in Slovakia has €1.4bn AUM, but operates only in Eastern Europe (Slovakia, Poland, Croatia, Czech Republic, Hungary), with EuroPension representing roughly 4% of their book.

Acquisition signal · the UK precedent
NatWest acquired Cushon
£144M · 2023
UK workplace savings and pensions fintech, acquired by the banking group to access modern pension UX, a pre-built licence, and a younger customer base.
JPMorgan acquired WealthOS
undisclosed · 2026
UK pensions administration and wealth-tech platform, acquired to gain pre-built infrastructure rather than build it from scratch internally.
Incumbents are turning to acquisitions to close the technology gap they cannot close organically. The pattern is expected to extend to Ireland and continental Europe as digital-first pension entrants reach scale.
