4.2 · Segmentation and channels

Sell in order of resistance. Net-new buyers first, switchers last.

Resistance to switching, not employer size, dictates the sales sequence. Accruo opens with employers who have nothing to defend, uses them to validate the product and the playbook, and earns the credibility needed to displace incumbents at the larger end. Each stage funds the next.

Segment ladder · three stages, three buyer types
Stage 1 · Y1 to Y2

Net-new buyers.

Founder-led startups and SMEs without an existing pension scheme. Hiring fast, no incumbent broker to displace, decisions made by one or two people, sales cycles in days. Small per-account LTV but rapid logo accumulation and product validation. The lighthouse cohort that proves the playbook.

Stage 2 · Y2 to Y3

Auto-enrolment-mandated SMEs.

Small firms (sub-50 staff) facing My Future Fund auto-enrolment with no scheme to defend. The competitive frame here is the default state scheme, not a broker. Little to lose, a lot to gain: the segment most receptive to switching to a better experience because they have never had one. B2C savers acquired in parallel.

Stage 3 · Y3+

Switching incumbents.

Mid-market and larger employers with existing broker-led schemes. Slowest cycle, multi-stakeholder, but the segment behind the €91k to €4m LTVs from the previous section. Earned credibility from Stages 1 and 2, plus direct distribution displacing brokers at the €100m AUM threshold, unlocks this.

Stage 1 funds the playbook. Stage 2 funds the team. Stage 3 funds the platform. Stages overlap rather than substitute, but the centre of gravity shifts year by year.

Channel cross-pollination, not channel replication. One ad spend, three buyer paths.

The HR lead scrolling LinkedIn during the week sees the B2B ad. The same HR lead scrolling Instagram on the weekend sees the B2C ad, and so do her employees. We pay once for B2C reach and capture three buyer paths into the same firm. Pure-B2B competitors cannot do this.
