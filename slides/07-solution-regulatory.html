Regulatory design at the core.

Most Irish pensions are sold as insurance contracts and run as collective employer schemes. Accruo makes the opposite choice on both, and the saver gets a better product for it.

The pension that moves with you

Accruo enters the market with the Personal Retirement Savings Account (PRSA), an individually owned pension, instead of the collective master trust schemes incumbents run.

Why the PRSA
  • Personal account. Owned by the saver, not the employer. Stays with them across job changes.
  • Workplace-eligible. Employers can adopt the PRSA as their workplace pension, and small employers are legally required to have a PRSA provider, even if they're in the government's auto-enrolment scheme.
  • Drawdown stays with us. At retirement, savers draw down directly from the PRSA.
Our discussion with the Head of Investments at Bank of Ireland
“Larger corporate clients raise post-retirement support as a real concern. Master trusts don't permit drawdown, so when they're evaluating a scheme they want to know there's a credible exit path for their members.”

The EuroPension is structurally the same individual-account product, so when we expand we'll already have the infrastructure and licence. We launch on the PRSA because the EuroPension can't currently accept transfers from existing Irish PRSAs. That constraint doesn't exist in other EU markets, and ongoing reforms may close it for Ireland too.

Plain language, more customisation

An Accruo statement will show your shares in the Vanguard S&P 500 fund, where the incumbents show units in a unit-linked Global Equity policy linked to the holdings in the Vanguard S&P 500. And as we move beyond our MVP, we'll be able to offer our clients more customisation than incumbents can match.

This is structural. We run the pension as a MiFID custody account, where clients directly own their assets. Incumbents wrap pensions as insurance products under Solvency II, a regime designed for insurance risk. As an added bonus, MiFID regulatory capital and ongoing operational burdens are lighter too.

Ireland over alternative licensing jurisdictions

We considered other jurisdictions, such as Lithuania, known for start-up-friendly licensing. Ireland wins on four counts: a regulatory environment that has become markedly more pro-business in recent years, a competitive corporate tax structure with start-up rebates, a promising launch market with auto-enrolment behind it, and an Irish licence that carries a trust premium an Eastern European licence does not.

Our discussion with the CEO of NBC Global Finance
“Five years ago, the Central Bank of Ireland wasn't easy to work with. We're in the final stages of our banking licence now, and they're more competent and more business-friendly than they were. I was speaking with Monzo's Irish CEO recently and he said the same thing. The process is rigorous, but efficient. It's a good time to launch a firm in Ireland.”
