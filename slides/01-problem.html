1.1 · The Problem

A retirement crisis, and a competitiveness crisis. One root cause.

State pensions are under pressure. Ageing populations are straining pay-as-you-go systems, and governments are shifting the burden of saving for retirement onto individuals. But individuals aren't filling the gap. Only 20% of EU citizens participate in a workplace pension.1

Europe has a parallel challenge. The Draghi report on EU competitiveness identified an €800 billion annual investment gap Europe must close to fund its infrastructure and industrial renewal. The report argued this gap can only be closed through investment provided by retirement savings.2

Both problems point to the same fix. Europeans need retirement products that put their money to work, and today's don't deliver. Europeans save €1.4 trillion a year, but €10 trillion, around 70% of household savings, sits in low-yield deposits.3 The small share that does reach pensions is eroded by fees averaging 2% in Europe versus the 0.26% average in the US,4 compounding into a 30% smaller retirement pot.5

Saving for retirement is a poor experience.

“When I joined a multinational in Ireland, nobody could explain my workplace pension. My HR told me to speak to the broker, who took weeks to arrange a meeting. Fund options arrived on five separate sheets of paper with no fee comparison. Enrolment required scanning paper forms. And when my employer improved their contribution — effectively a pay rise — the change was buried in jargon nobody understood. I thought, surely, this could be better.”

— Callum, Co-Founder of Accruo

And it's not just Ireland. This experience repeats across Europe, where neither legacy providers nor brokers have innovated to earn their fees. The consequences fall on the individuals who bear the cost, and the HR teams left managing the complexity.
